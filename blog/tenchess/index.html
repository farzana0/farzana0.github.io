Looking at Chess Through Tensor Decompositions

What happens if you represent a chess game as a tensor instead of a sequence of moves?

Chess is almost always written down as a sequence: position, move, position, move — a game unspooling one ply at a time, like a sentence. The notation is so natural that it's easy to forget it is a choice. But very little of what matters in chess lives on a line. A knight's influence spreads over a grid of squares; a pawn structure is a shape, not a list; two armies press against each other in space while the position drifts through time. Stack the snapshots of a game on top of one another and what you are holding looks less like a sentence and more like a solid block of structured data — ranks, files, piece types, time, each along its own axis.

A block with axes is a tensor. So I wondered what happens if you take that reading seriously: represent a position as a stack of 8×8 planes, one per piece type and color, and stack those boards along a time axis. It is a small change in viewpoint, but it makes an entirely different set of mathematical tools available — low-rank decompositions, multilinear surrogate models, structured factorizations. None of these has anything to do with playing better chess; the point isn't engine strength. The point is understanding structure: does chess, viewed this way, have any?

Before that toolbox can tell you anything, there's a question that has to come first: does the multilinear view preserve the game, or does it flatten it into something that merely looks like chess? So the first experiment was deliberately modest. I took a handful of classic games, defined some simple position-evaluation functions (material, mobility, king safety), and asked three plain questions. Can a small multilinear model faithfully stand in for a chess value function? Do the importance scores you read off such a model point at strategically relevant squares? And do low-rank decompositions of a whole game find coherent temporal structure, or just noise?

The first observation is the one everything else rests on. If you treat the pieces of a position as things that can be present or absent — mask a subset, ask the evaluation how good the position still is — you induce a function over piece subsets. I fit a rank-4 multilinear surrogate to a small sample of these masked positions, and it reproduced the evaluation on held-out maskings almost exactly, position after position. I expected decent fits; I did not expect near-perfect ones. And the natural worry — that a flexible surrogate would fit anything — has a direct answer: when I replaced the evaluation with a seeded random function of the position, the same machinery could not fit it at all. The structure being captured is in the game, not in the curve-fitting. Whatever else is true, these evaluation landscapes are genuinely low-rank multilinear objects, which is exactly the property the tensor toolbox needs.

Because the surrogate is faithful, the importance scores it implies (in the spirit of Shapley values, if you know that literature) actually mean something. Here the result is encouraging, though far from uniform: at famous tactical moments, the squares the attribution emphasized overlapped the squares involved in the combination noticeably more often than chance — and the move actually played in the game tended to sit near the top of the induced move ranking, most strikingly at queen sacrifices. The same analysis under the random-control evaluation showed nothing at all, which is what suggests the alignment is tracking real game structure rather than an artifact of the method. The signal is uneven — sharp at forcing moments, weak at quiet preparatory moves — and I want to be careful not to claim more than that.

The third observation is more modest: decomposing whole-game tensors into a few components produced coherent, piecewise temporal factors — stable regimes separated by clean transitions — rather than the unstructured noise I half expected from so crude an encoding. What the components latch onto first is as much the bookkeeping of the representation as the drama of the game, which is itself a useful lesson: the encoding shapes what any decomposition can see. But games, viewed as tensors, are evidently far from random along the time axis.

Not everything worked. Some analyses came back weak or inconclusive, and a few behaved exactly the way negative controls should. That's the expected texture of exploratory work; the goal was never to validate every idea, but to find out which directions deserve real effort.

I have started pulling on several of these threads, and the early returns are promising enough to deserve their own write-ups. Constructed tactic positions — where you can verify by the attack maps alone that a pin or a fork is present, and break it by moving a single piece one square — look like they could give interaction-attribution methods something they chronically lack: unlimited ground truth about which pieces genuinely interact, and at what order. Endgame tablebases, being exact records of perfect play over a complete state space, are a natural place to ask how much multilinear regularity optimal chess actually contains. And the internal activations of a trained chess network are literally tensors over the board, which makes them an obvious target for the same decompositions, provided one is careful to compare everything against an untrained network of the same shape. Each of these deserves more space than a paragraph, so they will come separately.

What I take away is not that tensor methods are "the right representation" for chess. The experiment can't establish that, and I don't believe it yet myself. What it suggests is narrower and, to me, more interesting: the multilinear view exposes questions that are hard to even pose in the sequential one. Can strategic motifs emerge as tensor components? Do the interactions between pieces have a measurable order — are some tactics intrinsically pairwise and others intrinsically three-way? Can decompositions across many games reveal recurring strategic phases? I find those questions more interesting than anything in the experiment itself, and that seems like the right reason to keep going.
