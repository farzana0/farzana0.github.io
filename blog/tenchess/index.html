TenChess: Do Tensor-Network Explainability Tools See Anything in a Chess Game?

A pre-registered research prototype: chess games as tensors, Shapley-style attribution with negative controls, tensor decompositions of games, tablebases, and a real chess net's activations — with the failed hypotheses reported as plainly as the confirmed ones.

This is a research sketch, built and run in one overnight session on CPU. Every number and figure below regenerates from one script (./run_all.sh, seed 0) in minutes; hypotheses, metrics, and thresholds were frozen in pre-registration files before each study ran, and the annotations used as ground truth were fixed before any attribution code existed. Nothing here should be read as more than a first probe — but it is an honest one.

1. Chess as a tensor testbed

A chess position is naturally a tensor: an 8×8 board with 19 feature planes (piece occupancies, side to move, castling rights, en passant, halfmove clock). Less obviously, each move is a sparse affine operator on the flattened position — vec(X′) = A·vec(X) + b, with A a 1216×1216 sparse matrix relocating an occupancy bit and b carrying the constant-plane updates. The repository verifies this identity exactly on every move of every bundled game, including castling, en passant, and promotions (max error ~4·10⁻⁹).

That framing makes chess a cheap, fully transparent testbed for tensor-network explainability tools: coalition games over squares are small enough to enumerate or sample properly, ground truth exists (famous tactics, tablebases, machine-verifiable motifs), and everything runs on a laptop. The question is never "can we explain chess" — it is "do these tools track anything chess-meaningful, or are they noise?" A well-documented negative answer counts as success.

2. Study 1 — square attribution on famous games

Six public-domain pre-1900 games (the Immortal, the Evergreen, Morphy's Opera game, Légal's mate, Paulsen–Morphy 1857, Steinitz–von Bardeleben 1895), with 15 key tactical moments annotated before any attribution code ran. Players are the occupied non-king squares; the coalition game masks pieces (three baselines: empty-square, modal-board, permuted-piece); values come from cheap heuristics (material, mobility, king safety, a combined blend) plus a seeded random value function as negative control. Attribution is TN-SHAP-style: fit a rank-4 multilinear (CP) surrogate to 1,500 sampled coalitions, read exact Shapley values off it by dynamic programming; Monte-Carlo Shapley with standard errors runs alongside as a check.

H1 (attributions concentrate on tactic squares) — failed as pre-registered. Mean top-k overlap with annotated squares was 0.243 vs 0.140 under a random-draw null, but the registered aggregate (Stouffer's Z over per-moment permutation p-values) came out at p = 1.0. The reason is instructive: at 6 of 15 moments the overlap was exactly zero, and with a discrete statistic those moments carry p = 1.0, which Stouffer converts into large negative evidence that swamps the successes (best moment: the Immortal before 18.Bd6, overlap 0.6, p = 0.025). Pre-registering Stouffer on discrete permutation p-values was a specification mistake — but it was the registered analysis, so the verdict stands. An exploratory aggregate-mean test gives p = 0.0084 (heuristic) vs p = 0.68 (random control): a real but weak, inconsistent signal — attribution finds queen-sacrifice moments and completely misses quiet preparation moves.

H2 (negative control) — behaved. Under the random value function there is no concentration on annotated squares, and the rank-4 surrogate that fits the heuristic games at held-out R² ≥ 0.997 cannot fit the random game at all (R² ≤ 0.14). The pipeline does not invent structure where there is none — which is what licenses reading anything else at face value.

H4 (stability across masking baselines) — confirmed (mean Spearman ρ = 0.62 > 0.5), with a caveat worth knowing: removal-style baselines agree well with each other (empty↔permuted ρ = 0.88) while the modal-board baseline — which can replace a piece with a different one — is the outlier (ρ ≈ 0.5). The choice between removal and replacement baselines is not cosmetic.

3. Study 2 — CP decomposition of the game tensor

H3 (temporal factors peak at material swings) — failed, informatively. Rank-4 CP temporal factors of G ∈ ℝT×8×8×19 were peak-picked at their largest one-step change; mean |Δmaterial| at those plies is 0.21 vs 0.75 at random plies — not just null but reversed. The audit explains it: peaks land disproportionately on castling moves (28%). In this encoding a capture changes 2 of 1,216 tensor entries while castling flips four 64-entry constant planes; CP chases Frobenius mass, locks onto the side-to-move parity oscillation and meta-plane steps, and is nearly blind to single-piece events. Decomposing a raw feature-plane encoding explains the encoding, not the game.

4. Study 3 — how compressible is perfect play?

An endgame tablebase is an exact value function on a complete state space — a rare object in ML — and its tensor rank is a crisp measure of how much multilinear structure perfect play contains. We generated KRvK and KQvK distance-to-mate tables from scratch by retrograde induction (validated hard: max mate-in-16/10 match theory; 200/200 greedy-DTM playouts mate in exactly the tabulated plies), reshaped them into 8⁶ tensors over factored coordinates, and swept tensor-train ranks against a label-shuffled control with identical marginals.

The secondary hypothesis (spectra differ between deep wins and shallow wins) split: +33% effective-rank difference for KRvK (supported), +11% for KQvK (not). We could not find prior work on tensor ranks of tablebases; treat this as a first data point, not a law.

5. Study 4 — tactics as ground truth for interaction indices

First-order Shapley is structurally the wrong tool for tactics: a fork is a labeled pair interaction, a pin a labeled triple. Existing interaction benchmarks (shapiq, Faith-Shap) measure estimator convergence to the index — not whether the index recovers interactions that are semantically real. Chess supplies that ground truth mechanically: we constructed 50 knight-fork and 50 relative-pin positions plus matched controls with the tactic geometrically broken by one square, verified by attack predicates, never hand-labeled — and computed exact interaction indices from full coalition tables under a 2-ply material oracle.

  • Forks (pairwise) — failed: "both true pairs in the top 3" happened in 8% of forks vs 6% of controls (p = 0.23), for diagnosable structural reasons: the interaction is value-asymmetric (Black rescues the more valuable piece) and the substitution edge of the triangle competes for exactly the top-3 slots the criterion reserved.
  • Pins (third-order) — confirmed, strongly: the (pinner, pinned, shielded-queen) triple carries above-median third-order interaction in 96% of pin positions vs 46% of broken controls (p ≈ 10⁻¹²).
  • The control did its job by failing: under the random value function the joint two-pair statistic still fired (p = 0.009) — because the two "true" pairs share the knight, their indices are correlated, and our independence null for the joint statistic is anti-conservative. The single-pair statistic under the random function sits exactly at chance (p = 0.52). This flaw makes the fork test's null anti-conservative too, so its failure stands a fortiori; the pin test's within-position null is unaffected.
  • Budgets don't matter here: detection is flat from 4 to 128 Monte-Carlo samples per pair — the exact index already fails the fork criterion, so sampling is not the bottleneck.
  • Exploratory surprise: the static heuristic detects forks (rank-percentile p = 0.001) where the 2-ply look-ahead oracle does not — minimax realizes the fork's value (Black saves what it can), flattening the joint dependence that raw attack-geometry terms retain.

6. Study 5 — unsupervised decomposition of a real chess net

McGrath et al. showed human chess concepts are linearly decodable from AlphaZero with supervised probes. The open variant: does unsupervised tensor decomposition find concept-like structure without being told what to look for — and is it about training rather than architecture? Every analysis ran identically on a randomly initialized net of the same architecture; no trained-vs-random comparison, no claim. We hooked 4 depths of Maia-2 "rapid" (23M params, trained on human games) on 2,000 positions and Tucker-decomposed each (N, 8, 8, C) activation tensor.

Both pre-registered hypotheses were confirmed — with honest caveats. Concept alignment beats the permutation null and the random net in 18 (depth, concept) cells, but the dominant factor is essentially piece count (r = 0.89), an input statistic; genuinely chess-like concepts are present but weak (king safety r = 0.26 vs 0.07 random at the deepest tap). And the effective-rank result, while large (76% relative difference), could plausibly involve normalization effects — one net, one seed pair. Unsupervised factors find far less than supervised probes did.

7. Related work

Piece-level perturbation saliency for chess exists — SARFA (ICLR 2020) and a recent first-order piece-SHAP adaptation (2025) — so Study 1's contribution is the pre-registered evaluation, the negative control, and the low-rank fidelity finding, not the method. Interaction benchmarks (Faith-Shap, shapiq) measure estimator convergence; we could not find prior work using tactics as semantic ground truth for interaction order, nor tensor-rank analyses of tablebases. Concept probing in chess nets is supervised in prior work (McGrath et al.; the learned look-ahead line).

8. Limitations

  • Heuristic value functions — material dominates, so attribution largely rediscovers material; no engine was installed on the build machine.
  • Tiny data — six games, 15 moments, 100 constructed tactic positions, two 3-piece endings, one net. Nothing here estimates variance across chess in general.
  • Specification mistakes are reported, not repaired — the Stouffer aggregation (Study 1) and the correlated-pair null (Study 4) were both flawed in ways we only saw after the results; the registered analyses are reported unchanged, the diagnoses labeled exploratory.
  • Encoding choices — Study 2's failure is largely a statement about the 19-channel encoding; the piece-count confound hangs over Study 5's alignments.

9. Verdict

Across ten pre-registered hypotheses in five studies: confirmed — baseline stability (H4), pins as third-order interactions (B2), trained-vs-random activation structure (C1, C2); failed — tactic-square concentration (H1), temporal-material alignment (H3), strict tablebase low-rankness (A1), pairwise fork recovery (B1); one split (A2), one control-exposed null flaw (B3). The one-line summary: first-order attribution on heuristic values mostly re-explains material and raw-encoding CP mostly re-explains the encoding — but the coalition games are startlingly low-rank, third-order indices genuinely recover pins where pairwise indices structurally miss forks, perfect play is measurably more compressible than its shuffled shadow without being "low-rank", and training a real chess net collapses its activation spectrum onto a handful of weakly concept-tracking directions.

Prototype built and run autonomously overnight; all pre-registration files, decision logs, and the full run report live alongside the code.
