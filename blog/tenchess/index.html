A Pin Is a Three-Piece Object

Chess tactics are interactions with a definite order — and generated positions with matched broken controls let you check whether interaction-attribution methods actually recover them. Third-order indices find pins. Pairwise indices miss forks, and the reason is the best part.

Tactics have an order

Ask a chess player what a pin is and they'll describe three pieces on a line: a bishop, a knight that can't move, and the queen hiding behind it. Take away any one of the three and there is no pin. It isn't a property of the bishop, or of the knight, or of any pair — it is irreducibly a three-piece object. A fork looks like a pair at first (knight attacks two pieces), but hold that thought; it turns out to be stranger.

Now look at what we actually use to explain models. Shapley values — the workhorse of feature attribution, including my own TN-SHAP line of work — assign one number per feature. First order. Whatever a pin is, a vector of per-piece numbers is structurally the wrong shape for it. Interaction indices exist precisely to fix this, but they have a chronic evaluation problem: the benchmarks we have (shapiq, Faith-Shap) test whether an estimator converges to the index. Nobody checks whether the index recovers interactions that are semantically real, because for most domains nobody knows the true interaction structure.

Chess does. You can construct, programmatically and in unlimited quantity, positions where a tactic verifiably exists — the attack maps say so, no human labeling — together with matched controls where one piece is nudged a single square so the geometry breaks but the material stays identical. Piece-ablation SHAP on chess has been done (SARFA; more recently Spinnato's piece-wise SHAP); the angle here is different and, as far as I can tell, unoccupied: use tactics as ground truth for interaction order.

Pins are recovered at third order

The setup: 50 positions in which a white bishop pins a black knight against the black queen along a diagonal, and 50 controls in which the queen steps one square off the ray. A coalition game over the pieces (mask a subset, evaluate a 2-ply search over material), small enough that I compute the full 2n table and get interaction indices exactly — no estimator noise in the way.

The third-order index on the (bishop, knight, queen) triple beats the position's own median triple in 96% of pin positions and 46% of the broken controls. Same material, same piece set, one square of geometry — a fifty-point swing in whether the triple stands out. That is what "a pin is a third-order object" looks like when an estimator meets it. The comparison to the broken control is the evidence I trust here, precisely because both conditions run through the identical pipeline and differ by one square.

Forks are triangles, and that's why pairwise misses them

Forks were supposed to be the easy warm-up: knight attacks rook and bishop, surely the two (knight, target) pairs light up. They don't. Requiring both true pairs in the top three of all pairs by interaction magnitude fires in 8% of fork positions — barely above the 6% of broken controls. The exact interaction matrices explain why, and it's the most useful thing this project taught me.

A fork's signature is a triangle, not a pair of edges. The two knight–target interactions are there, but so is a third edge between the targets themselves — they are substitutes, since Black can only rescue one — and that substitution edge competes for exactly the top slots the detection criterion reserved for knight pairs. Worse, the triangle is value-asymmetric: Black saves the rook, so the realized joint effect concentrates on the knight-and-cheaper-piece pair while the knight–rook edge is genuinely weak. Pairwise indices aren't noisy here; they are faithfully reporting that a fork is not a pairwise object. If you want to detect forks, score the triple. That experiment is queued below.

One more thing I didn't expect: the static evaluator separates forks better than the 2-ply search does. Look-ahead realizes the tactic — in the searched game Black has already saved what it can — which flattens the very joint dependence the raw attack-geometry terms carry. The oracle you'd trust more for values is the worse oracle for interaction structure.

Why "tensor" is in the project name

The same coalition games explain why tensor-network surrogates (the TN-SHAP trick) are so effective on this kind of function: they are barely nonlinear. A rank-4 multilinear surrogate fit to 1,500 sampled coalitions predicts held-out coalition values of the hand-tuned evaluator at R² ≥ 0.997 across all fifteen test positions — while the same surrogate fit to a seeded random value function tops out at R² ≤ 0.14. When the function being explained really is low-rank multilinear, exact Shapley values off the surrogate come almost for free; and the negative control says the fit isn't an artifact of the fitting.

Perfect play compresses — up to a point

A different question with the same multilinear flavor: how much low-rank structure does perfect play contain? Endgame tablebases are exact value functions on complete state spaces, which makes them a rare clean target. I generated KRvK and KQvK distance-to-mate tables from scratch by retrograde induction — validated the paranoid way: the maxima land exactly on the known mate-in-16 and mate-in-10, and greedy play-outs from 200 random winning positions per table mate in exactly the tabulated number of plies — then swept tensor-train decompositions against a label-shuffled control with identical marginals.

The curve is the result. At every bond dimension the real tensor reconstructs 3–4× more accurately than its shuffled shadow — 7.1% vs 30% error for KRvK at 12.7× compression — so perfect play carries real multilinear structure that survives factorization. It never crossed the 5% error bar I'd fixed in advance, though, and I now think that bar was arbitrary: distance-to-mate is full of cliffs (move parity, king adjacency, the rook-cutoff geometry) that low-rank factorizations are exactly wrong for. Structured, yes. Low-rank, no.

Training collapses a chess net's activation spectrum

Last one, briefly. McGrath et al. showed human chess concepts are linearly decodable from AlphaZero with supervised probes. I wanted the unsupervised version: Tucker-decompose the activations of Maia-2 at four depths over 2,000 positions and see what the factors track — always against an identically-architected random-init net, because random convnets already produce spatially structured features and any claim that ignores that is empty.

Training collapses the spectrum: effective rank 1.0–1.9 across depths in the trained net against 2.8–4.7 at random init. And the surviving directions track hand-coded concepts above the control everywhere I looked — king safety at the deepest tap correlates at 0.26 vs the control's 0.07 — though I'll be blunt about the caveat: the dominant factor is essentially piece count (0.89), an input statistic, and unsupervised factors see far less than supervised probes do. The clean claim is the rank collapse; the concept story needs the piece-count confound regressed out before I'd lean on it.

What didn't work

Two studies came up empty, and they stay on the record. Shapley attributions over squares of six famous games do land on annotated tactic squares more than chance (mean top-k overlap 0.243 vs 0.140) — but the aggregation I'd committed to, Stouffer's Z over per-position permutation p-values, is brittle when a discrete statistic hands you exact p = 1 at six of fifteen positions, and the registered test failed; what signal exists is mostly material re-explained, concentrated at queen sacrifices and absent at quiet moves. And CP-decomposing raw game tensors segments games at castling, not captures — material change at the factor peaks is 0.21 vs 0.75 at random plies, because castling flips four constant 64-entry planes while a capture changes two cells. Decompose a raw encoding and you learn about the encoding. The complete accounting, including the two places my own registered analyses turned out to be mis-specified, is in the appendix below.

What's next

Four follow-ups, in order of how much I care: a corrected detection null that permutes tactic labels within positions (the original independence null is anti-conservative when candidate pairs share a piece — the random-value control caught this); third-order indices on the fork triangle itself, which the diagnosis above predicts should separate forks from controls the way triples separated pins; a skewer generator — an aligned triple like the pin with the values reversed — as a third tactic class at a known order; and the pin result rerun across every oracle in the codebase. Results will be appended here when they're in.

Methods, limits, record

Hypotheses, metrics, and thresholds for everything above were fixed in written pre-registrations before each experiment ran, ground-truth annotations before any attribution code existed; where a registered analysis turned out to be mis-specified, the verdict stands as registered and the diagnosis is labeled exploratory. Everything runs from one script with a fixed seed on a laptop CPU. The honest limits: the oracles are cheap heuristics and shallow searches, not engines; the data is six classic games, a hundred constructed tactic positions, two three-piece endings, and one network; and several thresholds (the 5% compression bar, top-3 detection) were educated guesses. The code, pre-registration files, decision log, and full run report are not yet public — publishing the repository needs an interactive GitHub session this machine doesn't have — so the appendix below carries the complete hypothesis-by-hypothesis record in the meantime.

Appendix: the full record

Every registered hypothesis with its criterion, outcome, and verdict. Nothing omitted.

  • H1 (square attributions concentrate on annotated tactic squares; Stouffer p < 0.05 and observed > null): mean overlap 0.243 vs 0.140, Stouffer p = 1.0 (six of fifteen zero-overlap positions dominate) — failed. Exploratory aggregate-mean test: p = 0.0084.
  • H2 (random-value negative control shows no concentration; p ≥ 0.05): mean overlap 0.123 vs 0.140, p = 1.0; surrogate cannot fit the random game (held-out R² ≤ 0.14 vs ≥ 0.997) — control behaved.
  • H3 (CP temporal peaks align with material swings; p < 0.05): reversed — |Δmaterial| at peaks 0.21 vs 0.75 at random plies; 28% of peaks are castling moves — failed.
  • H4 (attribution rankings stable across masking baselines; mean Spearman ρ > 0.5): ρ = 0.618 (empty↔permute 0.88, empty↔modal 0.51, modal↔permute 0.47) — confirmed.
  • A1 (tablebase TT error < 5% at > 10× compression, shuffled control > 2× real error): real 7.1%/9.3% (KRvK/KQvK) at 12.7×, shuffled 29–33% everywhere; 5% bar never reached — failed at threshold (control contrast large at every rank).
  • A2 (deep-win vs shallow-win spectra differ by > 25% effective rank): KRvK +33% (yes), KQvK +11% (no) — split.
  • B1 (both fork pairs in top-3 pairwise interactions; beats null, exceeds broken controls): 8% vs 6%, p = 0.23 — failed.
  • B2 (pin triple above median third-order interaction; rate > 0.5, exceeds controls by > 0.10): 96% vs 46% — confirmed.
  • B3 (random-value control shows no fork detection): joint statistic fired (p = 0.009) — failed as registered; root cause: the independence null is anti-conservative for pairs sharing a piece (the single-pair statistic sits at chance, p = 0.52). This flaw biases B1's null anti-conservatively, so B1's failure stands a fortiori; B2's within-position null is unaffected.
  • B4 (detection vs Monte-Carlo budget, descriptive): flat at 0.08–0.10 from 4 to 128 samples per pair; estimator bias at the largest budget 0.0035 pawns.
  • C1 (unsupervised Tucker factors align with hand-coded concepts, beating the random-init net; permutation p < 0.05): 18 (depth, concept) cells qualify; strongest chess-like alignment king safety 0.26 vs 0.07 — confirmed, with the piece-count (0.89) confound noted.
  • C2 (effective-rank-vs-depth profile differs from random init by > 25%): 1.0–1.9 vs 2.8–4.7, max relative difference 76% — confirmed.
